Team 2158 - ausTIN CANs

Team 2158 – the ausTIN CANs, was founded in 2007. Since being recognized with the Rookie All-Star Award, we’ve worked to transform our culture and inspire the next generation of innovators and engineers. As Austinites identifying with the famous motto:1 “Keep Austin Weird,” our team members collaborate to promote STEM in imaginative ways. Every year, the CANs introduce new educational opportunities, activities, and outreach that feed into Austin’s technology landscape. We support our local partners and teams, inspire community members to start new FIRST teams , and host competitions and other events. The culmination of our efforts cultivates STEM learning and strengthens FIRST’s message in our community.

Team 2158 Robot Design

Design Process

Opportunity Cost

Opportunity cost analysis means considering each mechanism that not only in terms of materials, but also in terms of time lost for the development of other mechanisms. Opportunity cost analysis is a way of measuring the worth of each mechanism not only in monetary terms, but in ways of determining cost of time. To examine our design process through opportunity cost, the design team assigns a value (1-3) for both contribution to scoring goals and difficulty in planning, testing, and fabrication. We then use a set of tables to weigh contribution to scoring against opportunity cost. Ultimately, time is not over-invested in mechanisms which present limited benefit to our scoring scheme.

Solidworks Materials Testing

Team 2158’s partnership with UT Austin and ongoing associate and professional level certification programs have allowed for a more advanced understanding of the Solidworks CAD software. This year, the team began material stress testing within Solidworks as part of routine design. The effectiveness of the intake arm struts was assessed through this technology.

Manufacturing

CNC Mill and CNC Lathe

Advanced CAD techniques have also impacted fabrication. The Can’s acquired CNC mill and lathe technology through partnership with the Austin Independent School District and Texas Workforce Commission, and depend on this technology to enact precise machining objectives, such as the placement of lightening holes in intake arm struts.

3D Printed Components

3D printing provides an advantage during design, by aiding in rapid prototyping efforts, but is also used on the Cans’ robot in the form of independent structural elements which were 3D printed to achieve particular shapes with relative rigidity and strength of material. The pneumatic holding tanks on the ‘Can-a-pult’ have been 3D printed.

Programming

The programming division for the ausTIN CANs works with LabVIEW, a language developed by National Innstruments. Our purpose is to help the drivers control the robot in an intuitive fashion, and to allow the robot to do as much as possible wihout need for human input. To do this, we learn how to interact with computational systems and how to devise logical steps to complete processes. Our members come from a variety of backgrounds: some have written code for years and pthers haev never touched an IDE in their lives, but all are welcome and accepted as part of our group. The difference in skill levels helps us to teach new coders quickly, while imporoving the advanced programmers' skills as well

Engineering Education

2158 expands STEM education within Anderson High School and the Austin Independent School District via the influence of FIRST. To grow the program, we secured $500,000 by impressing funders with our FIRST success, petitioning the school board, and registering for 501(c)3 status. Team-prompted reform at our school has impacted 200 students this year and nearly 700 total since 2009. Anderson students can move through 4 different 4-year robotics pathways with 13 course options. Independent Research and Design classes offer advanced participants a setting to undertake unique projects, from quadcopters and game development to audible calculators for the blind. Other courses include Electronics and STEM Entrepreneurship - a class where students undergo the process of creating and marketing a product. Students can also receive college credit and professional CAD certifications. More recently, we inspired an education foundation and the school district to fund construction of a new 10,000 sq ft engineering building at Anderson. Soon, up to 500 students each year will have access to technical instruction. This achievement contributes to our goal to make Anderson a hub for engineering education in Austin.

Members of 2158 are prepared for the future with the skills they acquire from our team and FIRST. In collaboration with UT Austin, one of our team members synthesized organic solar cells as an alternative energy source and designed an electrode that determines lead content in water. Another member conceptualized and prototyped a life support system designed for use in space and was awarded first place at the International Science Fair, research grants, and an internship at the AMES Research Center. His project is being redesigned for use on the International Space Station. Because of the impact of our team and FIRST, 88% of our 60 graduates study STEM fields at universities like Harvard, Cornell, Tufts, UT Austin, and the US Naval Academy. Many alumni remain active in FIRST by supporting the CANs, mentoring other teams, and volunteering at events.

We host CAN (Careers, Academics, and Networking) Talks to inform students about STEM careers and academics. These original presentations give insight into STEM degrees, career paths, and projects. Since July of 2012, we’ve hosted speakers from UT, National Instruments (NI), ARM, Qualcomm, and Apple. Students, community members, and local teams are all invited to attend CAN Talks, which we make accessible online.

Community Awareness & Outreach

ausTIN CANs use media and community events to spread FIRST’s message. Time Warner Cable included our president in a 2011 Connect A Million Minds PSA. American Airline’s magazine, American Way, wrote an article about our team and FIRST, spreading the message to their 8 million monthly fliers. We’ve been featured in Popular Mechanics and The Austin American Statesman, and appear often in local news reports. We visit the state capitol every two years to discuss STEM education with lawmakers. This past summer, in an unconventional pairing of engineering and the arts, we hosted a Robot Fashion Show with team 3320 at Barnes and Noble. We’ve participated in Austin’s Susan G. Komen Race for the Cure, DorkBot at SXSW, RoboBaskets Day with the Austin Toros Basketball team, and Austin Maker Faire. We estimate that we’ve introduced FIRST to at least 1.5 million people.

We hold Austin CAN! food drives at our events that benefit the Capital Area Food Bank, collecting 5000 canned goods over the past 3 years. These efforts previously supported the victims of the Bastrop wildfires. The team participated in the Austin CANstruction project, promoting the end of hunger in Central Texas by building the “Whale Tail” in memory of a former Anderson science teacher who passed away this fall. Our first can drive started at our end-of-build-season practice event, Austin Robot Roundup (ARR), now in it’s 7th year. ARR gives participants an opportunity to test-drive their creations and engage with other FIRST teams.

Entrepreneurship in Engineering

The ausTIN CANs provide students with real-world experiences. In 2012, we garnered support from Rackspace and 3-Day Startup to co-found High School Startup. High School Startup teaches teens the value of entrepreneurship within engineering by engaging them in the creation of a product. It takes students through phases of product development - brainstorming, market research, prototyping, and launch. Students crowdfunded their projects, including a new gearbox for robotics, a smartphone app for FRC scouting, and a musical instrument tuner. The gearbox group was featured on the Kickstarter homepage as a November 2012 Staff Pick; their project video was watched over 15,000 times. The success of the initial High School Startup is leading to its expansion and consultation for programs in San Antonio and San Francisco.

Growing & Strengthening FIRST

In the last seven years, we’ve hosted 25 robotics competitions and volunteered at countless more. We use FLL, FTC, and VEX qualifiers, FRC scrimmages, and other robotics events to grow and promote FIRST. We forged the Austin FTC community in 2007 with the creation and funding of the area’s first 14 teams and proceeded to nurture teams through donation of materials, events, workshops, and training. This year, we again supported the growth of FTC in our community by donating material valued at $20,000 to nearby schools. We encouraged our local middle schools to develop robotics programs to help found 24 VEX teams and donated VEX kits to UT Austin’s Robotics and Automation Society as part of an ongoing partnership. In conjunction with NI, UT, and Skillpoint Alliance, we hosted 3 training events for teachers interested in adding FLL and FTC to their classrooms. Over the past 5 summers, we’ve hosted week-long camps for students in grades 3-10, culminating in research presentations and competitions. Many of the 400 young campers we’ve introduced to FIRST have joined FRC teams or taken robotics classes in high school. Summer camps provide funding, long-term team sustainability, and growth while inspiring the next generation.

During the summer of 2011, we hosted the first annual Texas Robot Roundup, the first official Texas FRC off-season competition. Known as TRR, it has served as a model for new off-seasons in our state. NI, Time Warner Cable, FIRST in Texas, and the Alamo Region have partnered with our team to support the event. We work with our parent organization, mentors, and students, and many other local teams and professionals, all who serve as volunteers and provide an opportunity for teams throughout Texas and beyond to compete in the off-season. Teams can iterate their designs, try out new drivers, introduce new members, mentors, sponsors, and community members to FIRST, and attend workshops. Workshop topics include robot design, programming, team sustainability, non-engineering mentoring, strategy, and design panel discussions lead by representatives from Texas’ most successful teams to help strengthen the Texas FIRST community. TRR continues to attract more teams each year, growing from 27 robots in 2011 to 42 robots in 2013 and in 2014, we had 34 FRC robots and 10 FTC robots. This summer, we hosted TRR at NIWeek with the addition of FTC to the competitions.

TRR has been a platform for giving Texas teams a strong start. Weeks before the first TRR, we traveled to Killeen, TX, and helped the pre-rookies, Shadetree Mechanics Team 3999, to build a robot and compete at TRR. We provided access to parts, facilities, and expertise. Shadetree went on to win the 2012 Alamo Regional Rookie All-Star Award. Three more of the eight teams that the CANs assisted during their first year similarly received rookie recognitions: 2468, 2881, and 3320. Recently, TIN CANs members inspired VEX students at Cedar Park HS to volunteer at TRR and start rookie FRC Team 5052. This past summer, a new homeschool pre-rookie team from north Austin made their first FIRST appearance at TRR with a robot we helped them build during the off-season. Partnerships

We nurture partnerships with many companies who provide mentors, funds, materials, and educational experiences for the team, including NI, Texas Instruments , UT, Draker Energy, Oracle, Intel, and Google. We've developed partnerships with the Austin Police Department and quadcopter innovators DJI, to design, assemble, and program our own quadcopters. Our connection with NI, which began when they paid our rookie year entry fee, grew into a mutually beneficial partnership. We were the first group to test NI's MyDAQ for integration into school curriculum and were featured in NI's STEM education videos. In addition to yearly invitations to demo robots at NIWeek, we were a 2011 Keynote Speaker, presenting an innovative knee brace design. Our members, working with UT students and Austin Regional Clinic, used FRC materials and NI products to solve a medical challenge, gaining valuable real world experience. In 2013, we worked with Waterloo Labsand teams 2468 and 2881 to build a robot waiter for NIWeek. This interteam-business partnership promoted FIRST to NIWeek attendees.

CAN Talks

The ausTIN CANs host monthly CAN Talks – educational TED-like events with industry professionals sharing their stories with students and community members. The talks are open to the public and recorded, then published online in our CAN talks playlist. These Career, Academic, and Networking talks have exposed students to dozens of personal, informative presentations that help prepare them for a career in STEM or otherwise.
